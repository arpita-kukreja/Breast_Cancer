Understanding Breast Cancer

Breast cancer affects 1 in 8 women worldwide, making it the second most common cancer in women. However, when detected early, the 5-year survival rate exceeds 99%. Regular screening and awareness of changes in your breast tissue are your most powerful tools in the fight against breast cancer.

Early detection saves lives. Learn about prevention, understand the warning signs, and get your mammogram analyzed with our AI-powered screening tool.

What is Breast Cancer?

Breast cancer occurs when cells in breast tissue grow out of control, forming tumors that can invade surrounding tissues or spread to other parts of the body. It's the most common cancer among women globally, but it can also affect men. The good news? Advances in detection and treatment have dramatically improved survival rates - especially when caught early.

Risk Factors

While some risk factors can't be changed, understanding them helps with early detection:

  • Age: Risk increases with age (most diagnoses after 50)
  • Family history: Having relatives with breast/ovarian cancer
  • Genetic mutations: BRCA1, BRCA2, and other hereditary factors
  • Dense breast tissue: Makes detection more challenging
  • Hormonal factors: Early menstruation, late menopause
  • Lifestyle factors: Alcohol use, lack of physical activity

Prevention & Screening

Early detection is your best defense. Follow these guidelines:

  • Annual mammograms: Starting at age 40-50 (consult your doctor)
  • Monthly self-exams: Know your normal breast tissue
  • Clinical breast exams: Regular check-ups with healthcare provider
  • Maintain healthy weight: Obesity increases risk
  • Exercise regularly: 150+ minutes moderate activity weekly
  • Limit alcohol: No more than 1 drink per day
  • Healthy diet: Rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains

Warning Signs

Contact your doctor if you notice any of these changes:

  • New lump: In breast, underarm, or around collarbone
  • Size/shape changes: Breast appears different than usual
  • Skin changes: Dimpling, puckering, or "orange peel" texture
  • Nipple changes: Discharge (other than breast milk) or inversion
  • Skin irritation: Redness, scaling, or thickening
  • Pain: Persistent breast or nipple pain (though most lumps aren't painful)

Remember: Most breast changes are NOT cancer, but early evaluation is always recommended.
